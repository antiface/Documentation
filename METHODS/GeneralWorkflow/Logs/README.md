#### Potential practices
* Keeping several types of interlinked/interconnected logs can be a useful form of documentation of the processes of an organization;
* A log can have many data types, it can take on or contain many different data structures;
* Logs are always timestamped/datestamped;
* Try to be as regular as possible in your logging practices;
* Do reviews and "clean_ups" when you do backups;
* Keep some physical logfiles, journals, dossiers, etc.;
* Index cards are still a useful form of documentation;

__LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS (LLMs) AND LOGFILES OF WEBSITES VISITED:__
* I've literally been keeping track of every website I visit for the last 10 years;
* What I do is that I surf the web, adding new tabs all the time, and when I get to about 20-30 tabs, I save them as a list in a Gist file on GitHub Gists as well as on my computer in a file with filename *"LOG_datestamp.md"*, i.e. __"LOG_2025.md"__;
* I save the __*"TabSets"*__, I call them, and then collapse the tabs and start the process over again from the beginning;
* I've been keeping logs of all of the web resources I access at first on just one computer but not that diverged to two computers;
* In any case, I just decided to take the logfiles of my web visits/surfing and use Large Language Models to extract data from them;
* What I did is I gave the logfiles, piece by piece, into the LLM and got it to extract data from the titles of the web pages/resources;
* In the Mardown files (.md) in which I keep my logs of my TabSets-over-time, with timestamps for each new Tab-Set I log, I have the titles of each page, so I got the LLM to take the titles and do a kind of content analysis of what my intellectual pursuits were at those different times;
